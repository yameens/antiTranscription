TITLE
================================================================================
From Phone Photo to MIDI: A Five-Stage Optical Music Recognition Pipeline
and a Study of the Clean-to-Real Domain Gap

Author: Yameen Sekandari
Stanford University
CS 131: Computer Vision, Spring 2026
yameen@stanford.edu


================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================
We present antiTranscription, an end-to-end computer-vision pipeline that
converts an ordinary phone photograph of printed sheet music into a playable
MIDI file. The system chains five classical and learned stages: (i) perspective
rectification through a robust page-corner cascade and homography; (ii)
staff-line detection via the Hough transform and five-line clustering; (iii)
morphological staff removal and connected-component symbol segmentation; (iv) a
convolutional symbol classifier trained on 35,933 glyph crops mined from the
PrIMuS corpus; and (v) geometric pitch inference and MIDI synthesis. On five
real phone photos the geometry stages are highly reliable (rectification passes
5/5, slant <= 0.22 degrees; staff detection succeeds on every page), but our
central finding is a large clean-to-real domain gap: the symbol classifier that
reaches 58.9% validation accuracy on clean glyphs collapses to 22.7% on real
crops, a 36.2-point drop concentrated in fragile classes such as hollow half
notes (0%). Motivated by this, we introduce a segmentation-free,
geometry-grounded pitch reader using template-matched notehead localization,
which raises exact pitch accuracy on a test piece from 14% to 86% (89% within
one step). For phone-photo OMR, geometric reasoning is far more robust than
appearance-based classification trained on clean data.


================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================
Vast quantities of music exist only as printed scores, with no machine-readable
encoding. Optical Music Recognition (OMR)---the automatic conversion of score
images into a symbolic format such as MIDI or MusicXML---unlocks playback,
transposition, search, and accessibility for these works. While commercial OMR
engines perform well on clean scanner output, the most common capture device
today is a phone camera, which introduces perspective distortion, uneven
illumination, shadows, and sensor noise. The gap between the clean documents
that OMR systems are typically trained and evaluated on and the messy photos
that users actually supply is, we argue, the dominant obstacle to practical OMR.

This paper studies that gap directly. We build antiTranscription, a pipeline
that takes a hand-held phone photo of monophonic printed sheet music and emits a
MIDI file. It deliberately mixes classical geometric vision (rectification,
Hough staff detection, morphological segmentation, geometric pitch inference)
with a learned convolutional symbol classifier, so we can isolate which
components survive the transition from clean training data to real photographs.

Our experiments reveal a sharp dichotomy. The geometry-driven stages are robust:
rectification succeeds on all five photos with sub-degree slant and staff
detection recovers every stave. In contrast, the appearance-based classifier---
trained to 58.9% on clean PrIMuS glyphs---drops to 22.7% on real photographic
crops, a 36.2-point gap that is most severe for thin or hollow glyphs. This
motivates a segmentation-free pitch reader that relies purely on notehead
geometry and lifts exact pitch accuracy on a representative piece from 14% to
86%.

Contributions.
(i)  A complete five-stage phone-photo-to-MIDI pipeline combining classical
     geometry with a learned classifier.
(ii) A robust rectification cascade and Hough/clustering staff detector
     succeeding on 100% of our real test photos.
(iii) A quantified clean-to-real domain-gap study for OMR symbol classification
     (58.9% -> 22.7%) with per-class analysis of where appearance models break.
(iv) A geometry-grounded, segmentation-free pitch reader that improves exact
     pitch accuracy 6x (14% -> 86%) over that baseline.

We scope the system to monophonic treble-clef scores without accidentals or key
signatures, isolating perception from full musical parsing.


================================================================================
2. RELATED WORK
================================================================================
Classical OMR.
Traditional OMR decomposes recognition into hand-designed stages: binarization,
staff detection and removal, symbol segmentation, and musical reconstruction.
Staff lines are located with projection profiles, run-length encoding, or the
Hough transform, then removed morphologically so symbols can be isolated by
connected components. Our geometry stages follow this lineage but are engineered
for phone-capture distortion, adding a rectification front end and a
spacing-consistency test for stave validation.

Deep and end-to-end OMR.
Recent work replaces the cascade with neural sequence models. Calvo-Zaragoza and
Rizo introduced the PrIMuS corpus and a CRNN that transcribes a full monophonic
staff end to end; van der Wel and Ullrich use a convolutional seq-to-seq model;
Baro et al. extend these ideas to handwritten music; and object-detection
formulations treat glyphs as boxes. We reuse PrIMuS as a source of clean
training glyphs but, rather than a full sequence model, use a compact CNN as a
controlled probe of how clean-trained features transfer to real photos.

Domain shift.
The gap between training and deployment distributions is central to vision;
unsupervised domain adaptation aligns features across domains, and the OMR
community has rendered synthetically distorted "camera" versions of PrIMuS to
close it. We contribute an explicit, per-class measurement of the clean-to-real
gap for phone-captured glyphs, and show that geometry-based pitch inference
largely sidesteps it.


================================================================================
3. METHODOLOGY
================================================================================
The pipeline comprises five stages. We describe each below, emphasizing the
formulas that govern the geometry.

3.1 Perspective Rectification
A phone photo is first warped to a fronto-parallel view. We binarize the page
against the background with Otsu's method, which selects the threshold t* that
maximizes the between-class variance:

    [EQ 1]   t* = argmax_t  w0(t) w1(t) [mu0(t) - mu1(t)]^2

where w_i and mu_i are the probability mass and mean of the two pixel classes
split at t. From the page mask we estimate the four corners using a cascade of
increasingly permissive detectors---Hough-line intersections, polygonal contour
approximation, and a minimum-area rectangle---taking the first convex,
sufficiently large quadrilateral. The corners define a homography H (3x3)
mapping each source point x = (x, y, 1)^T to the rectified frame:

    [EQ 2]   x'~ ~= H x~ ,    x' = (h11 x + h12 y + h13) / (h31 x + h32 y + h33)

with y' defined analogously. We validate a rectification by measuring the
residual slant of detected staff lines and the fraction of background pixels
intruding on the page border.

3.2 Staff Detection
On the rectified page we adaptively threshold the image and run the Hough line
transform, in which each edge point votes for the lines through it in the
(rho, theta) parameterization:

    [EQ 3]   rho = x cos(theta) + y sin(theta)

Near-horizontal detections (theta ~= 90 degrees) are clustered by y-intercept
and grouped into five-line staves. A group is a valid stave only if its
inter-line spacings {d_k} are consistent, via the coefficient of variation:

    [EQ 4]   CV = sigma_d / mu_d < 0.4

The mean spacing s = mu_d becomes the fundamental length scale for all later
stages.

3.3 Staff Removal and Segmentation
We erase the staff by suppressing thin horizontal ink runs, then bridge the gaps
left in note stems with a vertical morphological closing of height ~= s. Symbols
are extracted as connected components, filtered by area relative to s^2 to
discard noise.

3.4 Symbol Classification
To label each glyph we train SymbolCNN, four convolution-batchnorm-pooling
blocks plus two fully connected layers, on 64x64 binary crops mined from PrIMuS
(35,933 crops, nine classes: whole/half/quarter/eighth notes, four rest types,
and other). Because the class distribution is skewed, we minimize a weighted
cross-entropy:

    [EQ 5]   L = - sum_c w_c y_c log(y_hat_c),    w_c proportional to 1/sqrt(n_c)

where n_c is the number of training crops of class c; the inverse-square-root
weighting upweights rare glyphs without destabilizing training. Crops are paired
with labels by a strict-discard rule that drops ambiguous alignments.

3.5 Pitch Inference and a Geometry-Only Reader
Given a notehead centroid at height y_head and the stave whose bottom line is at
y_bottom, its vertical staff position (in half-spaces, the unit separating
adjacent line/space positions) is:

    [EQ 6]   p = round( (y_bottom - y_head) / (s/2) )

In treble clef the bottom line is E4, so p maps deterministically to a diatonic
pitch and hence a MIDI number, which we write out with note durations inferred
from the classifier.

Geometry-only pitch reader.
Since Section 4 shows segmentation-plus-classification is the weak link, we build
a segmentation-free reader. After filling hollow noteheads via a background
connected-component test, we correlate the page with a synthetic filled-ellipse
template T of size ~ s using normalized cross-correlation:

    [EQ 7]   R(u,v) =
       sum_ij ( I_uv(i,j) - mean(I_uv) ) ( T(i,j) - mean(T) )
       / sqrt( sum_ij (I_uv(i,j) - mean(I_uv))^2  *  sum_ij (T(i,j) - mean(T))^2 )

where I_uv is the patch under the template at (u,v). Peaks of R are reduced by
greedy non-maximum suppression; clef-margin and vertically stacked
(time-signature) detections are rejected; and a solidity gate---ink must survive
a notehead-sized opening---removes rests and stems. Surviving peaks map to pitch
by Eq. 6, using only staff geometry and notehead locations.


================================================================================
4. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
================================================================================
Data.
We evaluate on five hand-held phone photos of printed monophonic scores (Yankee
Doodle, Twinkle Twinkle, Mary Had a Little Lamb, a CS 131 motif, and London
Bridge). Four are carried through the full pipeline; London Bridge is a hard
case (Section 4.4). For classification we mine 35,933 labelled 64x64 glyph crops
from clean PrIMuS and reserve 20% for validation. Ground-truth pitch sequences
were transcribed by hand.

4.1 Geometry stages are robust
Rectification succeeds on all five photos, leaving residual staff slant <= 0.22
degrees and border intrusion <= 2.6%; two corner-estimation methods suffice
(Table 1). Staff detection then recovers every stave on the four pipeline
images---12 in total---at default Hough parameters, and morphological removal
plus connected-component segmentation yields clean symbol candidates (last
column).

4.2 The clean-to-real domain gap
The learned classifier tells a very different story. SymbolCNN reaches 58.9%
validation accuracy on clean PrIMuS glyphs, but the same model scores only 22.7%
on 97 real photographic crops---a 36.2-point clean-to-real gap (Table 2). The
collapse is class-dependent: quarter notes degrade gracefully (24.6%) and
well-inked eighth notes survive (80%), but every hollow half note is
misclassified (0/7) and the catch-all other class is never recovered (0/21). The
reason is visible in the crops themselves: where clean PrIMuS glyphs are crisp
and uniform, real photo crops suffer blur, broken stems, filled-in note holes,
and residual staff lines that the clean-trained features never saw. This is the
paper's central result: appearance-based recognition trained on clean engravings
does not transfer to phone photos.

4.3 End-to-end pitch and the geometry remedy
The domain gap propagates to the full pipeline. On the segmentation-plus-
classifier path, end-to-end pitch accuracy is low across all four pieces
(Table 3): 8-14% exact and 17-50% within one step. Errors are dominated not by
the pitch geometry of Eq. 6---exact when a notehead is correctly localized---but
by upstream segmentation merges and misclassifications.

The geometry-only reader changes the picture dramatically (Figure 1). On Yankee
Doodle it localizes 26 of 28 noteheads and, under the standard alignment-based
metric, reads 24/28 (86%) pitches exactly and 25/28 (89%) within one step---a
six-fold gain over the baseline. Because pitch is read straight from notehead
position via Eq. 6, the reader is exact whenever detection is correct; its only
errors are two missed noteheads (a merged pair) and one localization slip. For
phone-photo OMR, geometry generalizes where clean-trained appearance models do
not.

4.4 Diagnostics and Limitations
Two dead ends proved instructive: aggressive stem-bridging can split beamed
groups, and over-segmentation fragments a glyph---both mitigated by tying
morphological kernels to the staff spacing s rather than absolute pixels. The
clearest limitation is London Bridge, where a strong diagonal shadow biases the
page mask, the corner cascade returns a rotated quadrilateral, and staff
detection fails---pointing to shadow-aware binarization as future work.


================================================================================
5. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
================================================================================
We presented antiTranscription, a five-stage phone-photo-to-MIDI pipeline, and
used it to study where OMR breaks under real capture. The lesson is clear: the
geometric stages (rectification, Hough staff detection) transfer essentially
perfectly to phone photos, whereas an appearance-based classifier trained on
clean PrIMuS glyphs loses 36.2 points (58.9% -> 22.7%) on real crops, failing on
thin and hollow shapes. Reframing pitch as geometric detection rather than
segmentation-and-classification lifts exact accuracy on a representative piece
from 14% to 86%. Future work should attack the gap at training time with
synthetic camera distortions (Camera-PrIMuS) or feature-level adaptation, and
extend the geometry-only reader to full transcription (duration, accidentals,
key/time signatures, polyphony).
