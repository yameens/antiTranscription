The original method

A five-stage pipeline: rectify the page, detect staves with the Hough transform, remove the staff lines and segment symbols by connected components, classify each crop's duration with a small CNN, and read pitch from notehead geometry. Its central finding was a clean-to-real domain gap — the CNN scored 58.9% on clean glyphs but only 22.7% on real photo crops, because segmentation merges destroy notes and clean-trained features don't survive blur, shadow and ink-bleed.

What we upgraded

We removed segmentation entirely and dropped in an end-to-end CRNN+CTC reader (CNN → BiLSTM → CTC) that maps a whole staff image straight to a token sequence — recovering pitch and duration jointly. We trained it on 15,354 PrIMuS treble staves with synthetic phone-photo augmentation (blur, uneven light, JPEG, ink-bleed), on the laptop's Apple GPU.

The takeaway

Reading the staff end-to-end — instead of cutting it into crops and labelling each — turns the learned model from the pipeline's weakest link into its strongest, recovering pitch and rhythm together and generalizing from clean training data to real phone photos.

Honest limitation: Mary Had a Little Lamb's durations (54%) lag — pitch is perfect but some note lengths are misread. Training was stopped early (epoch 6) to spare the laptop; a longer run should close this.
